Consumer impact, stated carefully

The customer paid for a work budget. New products now have a claim on it.

For someone who previously relied on the allowance mainly for Codex, using Work or Excel can reduce what remains for coding. Once the included amount is consumed, the practical choices can become: ration the work, wait for a reset, upgrade, or purchase credits where available.

This does not prove that OpenAI raised its margins or that every subscriber receives less value. It does establish that more products can compete for one paid resource and that extra usage can require additional spending.

The consumer position

No payment demanded. Fair value expected.

Users do not need to claim ownership of the model or demand wages for every conversation. The fair request is simpler: when paying customers help fund the service and may also contribute useful real-world data, new products should not quietly dilute the same paid work allowance.

Training can be disabled, Temporary Chats are excluded, and business products are opted out by default. It would be inaccurate to say every user's work trains every model.

What fair disclosure should look like

Five requested fixes

  1. Increase the allowance when new products are added, or provide separate pools.A new paid capability should not quietly consume the capacity customers already relied on elsewhere.
  2. Put the shared-pool warning beside every product entry point.Users should know before starting that Work or Excel can reduce the capacity remaining for Codex.
  3. Show estimated usage before execution and name the consuming product afterward.Long or scheduled tasks should not become a billing surprise.
  4. Provide separate pools or user-configurable budgets.Customers should be able to reserve purchased credits for a chosen product.
  5. Offer a fair review and refund process.Especially where customers could not reasonably understand that a newly introduced feature shared an existing allowance.
